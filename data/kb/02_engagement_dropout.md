# Engagement Dropout Intervention Guide

## Overview
Declining engagement is one of the strongest early indicators of academic and emotional distress. When a student who was previously active stops logging in, stops submitting work, or disappears from course activity, it rarely happens without reason. This guide helps advisors respond effectively when engagement data flags a student.

Research consistently shows that disengagement follows predictable patterns: first missing small deadlines, then avoiding online platforms, then stopping all academic contact. Each stage presents an intervention window. The earlier the contact, the better the outcome.

## Key Principles
- Treat disengagement as a signal, not a character flaw
- First contact should be warm and non-accusatory
- The goal of first contact is to open a conversation, not fix the problem
- Persist — students in distress often don't respond to the first outreach
- Address underlying causes, not just surface behaviors

## Step-by-Step Guidance

### Step 1: Gather Context Before Reaching Out
Review available data: when did engagement drop, how sharply, and after what event? A student who was active for 6 weeks then went silent may have experienced a specific trigger (exam failure, personal crisis, illness). Knowing this helps you personalize the outreach rather than sending a generic message.

### Step 2: First Outreach — Low Pressure, High Warmth
Send a brief, personal message (not an automated system email). Reference something specific: "I noticed you haven't submitted the Week 5 quiz and wanted to check in." Offer two easy response options: reply to the message, or book a 15-minute call. Do not mention consequences in the first contact.

### Step 3: Second Outreach — Escalate Warmth
If no response within 5 days, follow up with slightly more urgency but still warmth: "I haven't heard back and I want to make sure you're okay. Even a quick reply to let me know you're alright would help." CC the student welfare office if still no response.

### Step 4: Direct Welfare Check
If the student has not responded to two outreach attempts and has been absent for 2+ weeks, escalate to a formal welfare check through the Student Services or Welfare team. Provide documentation of your outreach attempts.

### Step 5: Reconnection Support
When the student does re-engage, focus on reconnection rather than catch-up. Discuss what support they need to return, not what they missed. Negotiate a manageable re-entry plan with the module team.

## Warning Signs
- Sudden drop in weekly login frequency (from daily to zero)
- Missing 2+ consecutive assessments
- No response to module forum posts or emails
- Accessing only passive content (videos) but not interactive elements
- Increasing time between sessions before complete disappearance

## Escalation Triggers
- Student has been absent for more than 3 weeks with no contact
- Prior welfare concerns on record
- Known personal circumstances (bereavement, family crisis, financial hardship)
- The student was previously responsive and suddenly stopped all contact

## Sample Language / Scripts
- "Hi [Name], I noticed you haven't been on the course platform recently and wanted to check in. There's absolutely no pressure — I just want to make sure you're doing okay. Is there anything I can help with?"
- "I can see things have been quiet for you on the course lately. That happens, and we can absolutely work through it together. Could we find 15 minutes this week to chat?"
- "I'm not following up about grades — I'm following up because I think it's important that you know someone noticed and someone cares."

## Resources and Referrals
- Student Welfare Officer — for multi-week absence with no contact
- Module tutor — for academic accommodation requests
- Financial Support — if financial stress is contributing factor
- Disability Services — if health condition is involved
