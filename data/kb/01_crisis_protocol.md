# Mental Health Crisis Protocol

## Overview
A mental health crisis occurs when a student's emotional or psychological state poses an immediate risk to their safety or the safety of others. Advisors and counselors are often the first point of contact when a student shows signs of acute distress, suicidal ideation, or self-harm. Responding calmly, quickly, and correctly can be life-saving.

This protocol applies to any situation where a student expresses thoughts of suicide, self-harm, harming others, or displays acute psychotic symptoms. Even if you are not a clinician, your role as a first responder is critical.

## Key Principles
- Stay calm — your composure helps regulate the student's emotional state
- Take all statements about self-harm seriously, even if said "jokingly"
- Do not leave a student in acute crisis alone
- Your job is to keep the student safe and connect them to the right help — not to diagnose or treat
- Confidentiality has limits: student safety always takes precedence

## Step-by-Step Guidance

### Step 1: Assess Immediate Safety
Ask directly: "Are you thinking about hurting yourself or ending your life?" Asking about suicide does not plant the idea — it opens the door for honesty. If the student says yes, assess for a plan: "Do you have a plan for how you would do that?" A student with a specific plan needs immediate intervention.

### Step 2: Stay Present and Validate
Say: "I'm really glad you told me this. What you're feeling is serious and I want to make sure you get the right support." Avoid minimizing ("things will get better") or problem-solving before the student feels heard.

### Step 3: Contact Emergency Services or On-Call Counselor
If the student is in immediate danger (has means, plan, and intent), do not attempt to manage this alone. Call campus emergency services or 999/911. If the risk is elevated but not immediate, contact the on-call mental health counselor. Stay with the student until help arrives.

### Step 4: Document and Debrief
After the situation is resolved, complete a critical incident report. Debrief with your supervisor. Self-care after a crisis contact is essential — these interactions are emotionally demanding.

## Warning Signs
- Direct statements about wanting to die or not wanting to exist
- Giving away valued possessions
- Sudden calmness after a period of depression (may indicate a decision has been made)
- Talking about being a burden to others
- Dramatic mood changes or withdrawal from all activities
- Searching for methods online or acquiring means

## Escalation Triggers
- Student has a specific suicide plan with means and intent
- Student has made a recent attempt
- Student is intoxicated and expressing suicidal ideation
- Student refuses help and is believed to be in immediate danger

## Sample Language / Scripts
- "I can hear that you're going through something really painful right now. Can you tell me more about what's been happening?"
- "I want to ask you something directly — are you having thoughts of hurting yourself?"
- "You don't have to face this alone. I'm going to help connect you with someone who specializes in this. I'll stay with you."
- "Your safety is the most important thing right now. Everything else can wait."
