# Referral Pathways and Escalation Procedures

## Overview
Advisors and tutors are not mental health clinicians. One of the most important skills in student support is knowing when and how to refer a student to more specialist services. A well-executed referral can be transformative. A poorly handled one — where the student feels passed off or abandoned — can damage trust and reduce their likelihood of seeking help again.

This document describes the referral landscape, when to escalate, and how to make referrals in a way that maintains the student's dignity and trust.

## Key Principles
- Referral is a connection, not a hand-off — stay involved after referring
- Always explain why you're referring and what the student can expect
- Get the student's agreement before making a referral where possible
- Warm referrals (where you personally introduce or accompany the student) have significantly better uptake than cold referrals
- Document every referral made, with date, receiving service, and student response

## Step-by-Step Guidance

### Step 1: Match the Referral to the Need
Identify what the student's primary need is before choosing a service. Is it emotional support, practical assistance, academic adjustment, or crisis intervention? Sending a student who needs financial support to counseling wastes both the student's time and the counselor's. Use the right pathway.

### Step 2: Explain the Referral
Before referring, explain: "I think it would really help you to speak with someone who specializes in [X]. They're much better placed than me to help with what you're describing. Would you be open to that?" Never make a referral without the student understanding who they're being sent to and why.

### Step 3: Make a Warm Referral
If possible, send an email introducing the student to the receiving service while CC-ing the student. This reduces the barrier of the student having to explain their situation from scratch and shows continuity of care. "I'm introducing [Student Name] who I've been supporting around [general concern]. I've encouraged them to make contact with your service."

### Step 4: Follow Up
Contact the student 1 week after the referral to check they made contact. If they haven't, explore the barrier — logistical, emotional, or practical — and help them overcome it.

## Referral Pathways by Need

| Student Need | Appropriate Referral |
|---|---|
| Suicidal ideation / self-harm | University Counseling (urgent) + Student Welfare |
| Acute mental health crisis | Emergency services + On-call counselor |
| Mild to moderate anxiety or depression | University Counseling Service |
| Grief or bereavement | Counseling or Chaplaincy Service |
| Financial hardship | Student Finance / Hardship Fund |
| Physical health concerns | Student Health Centre |
| Disability / learning difficulty | Disability and Dyslexia Service |
| Domestic abuse / relationship violence | Student Welfare + external specialists |
| Academic difficulty without distress | Academic Tutor / Learning Support |
| Accommodation crisis | Student Services / Housing Team |
| International student specific needs | International Student Advisor |

## Warning Signs That a Referral Is Needed Urgently
- Student discloses suicidal thoughts, plans, or recent attempt
- Student is visibly unable to function (not sleeping, eating, caring for themselves)
- Student discloses abuse, assault, or domestic violence
- Student expresses hopelessness with no plan to seek help independently
- Student is in legal difficulty affecting their studies or safety

## Escalation Triggers
- Student refuses referral but appears to be in danger — escalate to Student Welfare without consent if safety is at risk
- Referred student does not access service within 2 weeks — re-engage and consider more active support
- Pattern of multiple referrals with no engagement — may require a multi-agency meeting

## Sample Language / Scripts
- "What you're describing sounds like something a counselor would be really well-placed to help with. They're not just for crises — they work with students on exactly this kind of thing. Would you be open to giving it a try?"
- "I'm going to send a quick email to introduce you to [service]. You don't have to do anything — they'll reach out to you. Is that okay?"
- "I want to make sure I'm not just sending you off and leaving you to figure it out. I'll check in with you next week to see how it's going."
