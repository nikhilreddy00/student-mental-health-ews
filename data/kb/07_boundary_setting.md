# Professional Boundary Setting for Advisors

## Overview
Professional boundaries are the clear limits that define the role of an advisor in relation to a student. They protect both parties: the student is protected from inappropriate dependency or exploitation, and the advisor is protected from burnout, liability, and role confusion. Maintaining appropriate boundaries does not mean being cold or unhelpful — it means being clear, consistent, and honest about your role.

Advisors who blur professional boundaries — even with good intentions — often find that students become over-dependent, that their own wellbeing suffers, and that the student's long-term outcomes are actually worse because healthy independence is not being fostered.

## Key Principles
- Clarity prevents confusion — be explicit about your role and its limits
- Consistency is more important than flexibility — changing the rules based on how you feel creates unpredictability
- Your wellbeing matters — you cannot effectively support students if you are depleted
- Boundaries are not barriers — they create a professional relationship that is more helpful, not less
- If you feel uncomfortable, that is a signal that a boundary may be needed

## Step-by-Step Guidance

### Step 1: Define Your Role at the Start of a Relationship
At a first meeting, briefly explain what you can and cannot do: "I'm here to support you academically and can connect you with welfare and counseling services. I'm not a therapist, and I won't always be able to respond immediately — but I'll always respond, and I want you to feel supported."

### Step 2: Manage Availability Expectations
Be clear about response times and contact channels: "I typically respond to emails within 2 working days. My office hours are [X]. For urgent welfare concerns, please contact the Student Welfare Team directly." If a student contacts you outside of these parameters repeatedly, address it directly and kindly.

### Step 3: Redirect When Conversations Exceed Your Role
When a student begins discussing something outside your competence or role (e.g., detailed therapeutic exploration of childhood trauma), redirect compassionately: "I really appreciate you sharing that with me, and I can hear how much this is affecting you. I think you deserve to talk to someone who is really trained to help with this — would you be open to speaking with a counselor?"

### Step 4: Handle Personal Disclosure From You
Do not share personal experiences, opinions, or vulnerabilities with students. Avoid statements like "I went through something similar when I was a student." This shifts focus from the student to you and can blur boundaries. You can be warm and human without self-disclosing.

### Step 5: Address Boundary Violations Directly
If a student texts you personally, contacts you on social media, or becomes inappropriately demanding, address it clearly: "I want to keep supporting you, but I need us to stick to [email/meetings] as our main way of connecting. That's what works best for me to give you the support you need."

## Warning Signs (that a boundary may need reinforcing)
- Student contacts you multiple times per day outside office hours
- Student expects immediate responses at all times
- Student shares very personal information that goes beyond academic concern
- You find yourself thinking about a particular student excessively when not at work
- You feel unable to say no to a student's requests
- Student attempts to contact you on personal social media

## Escalation Triggers
- Student's behavior toward you becomes threatening or harassing
- You are experiencing significant personal distress related to supporting a student
- Another staff member raises concern about the nature of your relationship with a student

## Sample Language / Scripts
- "I really value our conversations, and I want to make sure they stay useful for you. I'm going to refer you to [counseling/welfare] for the parts of this that go beyond what I'm best placed to help with."
- "I noticed you sent me a message over the weekend. I completely understand that things don't wait for office hours, but I want to be honest — I'm not able to respond outside of working hours regularly. For urgent things, [welfare contact] is available."
- "I care about your wellbeing, and part of how I show that is by making sure I'm connecting you to the right kind of support rather than trying to do everything myself."

## Resources and Referrals
- Employee Assistance Program — for advisors experiencing burnout or distress
- Line Manager / Supervisor — for advice on managing complex student situations
- HR / Legal — for situations involving boundary violations by students
