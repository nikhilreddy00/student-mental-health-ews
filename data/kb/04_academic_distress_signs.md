# Recognizing Signs of Academic Distress

## Overview
Academic distress is not simply "doing badly on exams." It is a state where a student's psychological, emotional, or social difficulties are interfering with their ability to engage with their studies. Advisors who can recognize the early behavioral and emotional signs of academic distress are in a position to intervene before the situation becomes a crisis.

The OULAD research dataset identifies several behavioral features that predict dropout and withdrawal. These align closely with what counselors observe clinically: declining engagement, falling assessment scores, and withdrawal from course participation are the most reliable early signals.

## Key Principles
- Distress manifests differently — some students over-perform to cope, others disengage completely
- Academic distress and mental health distress are deeply intertwined
- Do not wait for a student to self-identify — proactive outreach is more effective
- One missed deadline is noise; two or more is a pattern worth noticing
- Behavioral signals are more reliable than emotional self-report early in distress

## Step-by-Step Guidance

### Step 1: Notice Behavioral Changes, Not Just Outcomes
The most reliable early indicators are changes in behavior, not absolute performance levels. A student who was submitting on time and now isn't, or who was logging in daily and now isn't, is more concerning than a student who has always struggled. Pattern change is the key signal.

### Step 2: Cross-Reference Data Sources
Check engagement data alongside assessment results. A student who is still engaging with content but not submitting may be anxious about performance. A student who has stopped all engagement entirely is at higher risk. Both need outreach, but with different approaches.

### Step 3: Consider Context and Timing
Distress spikes around high-pressure academic moments: assessment deadlines, examination periods, module registration, and results releases. If a student who was fine suddenly becomes disengaged around these points, the cause is often clear and the intervention straightforward.

### Step 4: Ask Directly
When meeting with a student, ask open questions about their experience: "How are you finding the course lately?" and "How have things been going overall?" Most students will disclose if asked genuinely. Follow up on vague or dismissive responses.

## Warning Signs
- Submission rate dropping below 50% after a history of regular submission
- Assessment scores declining 15+ points over consecutive assessments
- Platform engagement dropping below 1 login per week after prior daily activity
- Student stops participating in discussion forums after previously being active
- Multiple module withdrawals or unregistrations in a single semester
- Asking about withdrawal, deferral, or extension options repeatedly
- Appearing visibly distressed, tearful, or agitated in meetings
- Expressed hopelessness about academic outcomes ("There's no point anymore")
- Blaming themselves excessively for academic difficulty

## Escalation Triggers
- Student expresses that they want to leave university due to feeling unable to cope
- Student has missed more than 3 consecutive assessments with no communication
- Student reports significant personal stressor (bereavement, relationship breakdown, financial crisis) alongside academic decline
- Student shows signs of significant weight change, sleep disruption, or hygiene neglect

## Sample Language / Scripts
- "I noticed your recent submissions have been a bit different from how you started the course. Can you tell me what's been going on?"
- "A lot of students find this point in the year really tough. How are you coping with everything at the moment?"
- "I want to check in — not about grades, but about you. How are things outside of university going?"
- "When you say 'I'm fine,' I want to make sure I'm not missing something. Is there anything under the surface that's making things harder?"

## Resources and Referrals
- University Counseling — for emotional distress underlying academic difficulty
- Disability and Dyslexia Services — if undiagnosed learning difficulty suspected
- Financial Aid — if money stress is identified as contributing factor
- Module Tutor — for negotiating academic adjustments
- Student Union — for peer mentoring and practical support
