# Grief and Loss Support Protocol

## Overview
Grief is a natural response to significant loss — most commonly bereavement, but also loss of a relationship, loss of identity, loss of health, or loss of a sense of safety following trauma. For university students, grief can be profoundly destabilizing. The structured demands of academic life — deadlines, exams, attendance expectations — do not pause for grief, and many students try to "push through" without adequate support.

Students who are grieving may present to advisors in ways that look like disengagement or academic failure. Understanding grief as the underlying cause changes the intervention entirely.

## Key Principles
- There is no correct timeline for grief — avoid statements like "you should be over this by now"
- Grief is not a disorder — it is a natural process that requires space, not diagnosis
- The academic environment is often poorly designed for grieving students — your role includes advocating for flexibility
- Supporting a grieving student requires presence more than problem-solving
- Cultural and religious practices around death and mourning vary enormously — do not impose your own framework

## Step-by-Step Guidance

### Step 1: Create Space to Acknowledge the Loss
When you become aware that a student has experienced a bereavement or significant loss, acknowledge it directly before addressing any academic concerns: "Before we talk about anything else, I just want to say how sorry I am about [the loss]. How are you doing?" Do not move quickly to practical matters — even if the student wants to, follow their lead and check in emotionally first.

### Step 2: Understand the Nature of the Loss and Its Impact
Not all losses have the same impact. A student grieving the death of a parent or sibling is experiencing something categorically different from a student who has experienced a relationship breakdown — though both may need support. Ask gently about the loss, the student's support network, and what they need right now: "Do you have people around you? Is there anything the university can do to make this period easier?"

### Step 3: Facilitate Academic Flexibility
Work with the student to identify what academic adjustments would help. Options include: extension to all current deadlines, deferral of upcoming assessments, a leave of absence or interruption of studies, and retrospective mitigation for affected work. Explain all options without pressure: "There's no right answer — I want you to know what's available so you can choose what feels right."

### Step 4: Refer to Specialist Grief Support
Grief counseling is distinct from general counseling. Where possible, refer to a counselor or service with specific bereavement experience. Chaplaincy or faith-based support may also be important for students whose grief is intertwined with religious meaning-making.

### Step 5: Plan for the Long Term
Grief is not linear. Students who seem to be coping initially may struggle weeks or months later — particularly around anniversaries, exam seasons, or occasions associated with the person who died. Plan for ongoing check-ins and make clear that the door remains open: "You don't have to be in crisis to come and talk. Grief has its own timeline."

## Warning Signs
- Student becomes tearful or distressed when discussing topics unrelated to the loss (suggests pervasive impact)
- Student discloses they have not told anyone about the loss (isolation risk)
- Student is making high-risk decisions (withdrawing from university suddenly, leaving accommodation)
- Student's grief appears complicated by guilt, anger, or unresolved relationship dynamics with the deceased
- Student's functioning is significantly impaired 3+ months after the loss (possible complicated grief)

## Escalation Triggers
- Student expresses suicidal ideation in the context of grief ("I want to be with them")
- Student discloses they are using alcohol or substances to cope with grief
- Student has no social support network and is isolating completely
- Student cannot perform basic self-care (eating, sleeping, hygiene) months after the loss

## Sample Language / Scripts
- "I'm so sorry for your loss. I can't imagine how difficult this must be. Please don't worry about any academic matters right now — we can sort all of that out. How are you doing?"
- "There's no right way to grieve, and there's no timeline you need to follow. What matters to me is that you have the support you need."
- "I want to make sure the university is as flexible as possible during this time. There are a number of options — shall I walk you through what's available?"
- "Even if things seem okay now, grief can come in waves. My door is always open — you don't have to wait until things are bad to come and talk."
