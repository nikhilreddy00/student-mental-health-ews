# Trauma-Informed Advising Practices

## Overview
Trauma-informed care is an approach to student support that recognizes the widespread impact of trauma and integrates knowledge about trauma into policies, procedures, and practices. Many students arrive at university having experienced significant adverse experiences: childhood abuse, neglect, domestic violence, poverty, bereavement, war, or displacement. These experiences shape how students relate to authority figures, how they respond to stress, and how they experience the academic environment.

Advisors do not need to know a student's trauma history to use trauma-informed practices. The approach is about creating conditions of safety and trust that allow all students — but especially trauma survivors — to engage effectively.

## Key Principles
- Safety first: physical and emotional safety must precede any productive conversation
- Trustworthiness and transparency: be clear about your role, what you can and cannot keep confidential, and what will happen next
- Peer support: connect students with others who have shared experiences where possible
- Collaboration: share power with students rather than making decisions for them
- Empowerment: build on student strengths rather than focusing on deficits
- Cultural sensitivity: recognize how culture, gender, and identity intersect with trauma responses

## Step-by-Step Guidance

### Step 1: Create a Safe Physical Environment
The meeting space matters. Ensure seats are arranged so the student does not feel trapped or cornered. Have tissues available but not prominently displayed. Offer water. Keep the door position something the student can control if they feel unsafe.

### Step 2: Establish Transparency About Your Role
Begin meetings by explaining clearly: "I'm your [Class/Academic/Personal] Advisor. My job is to support your academic journey and your wellbeing. Our conversations are confidential, except if I'm concerned about your safety or the safety of others — in that case, I'd always tell you first what I'd need to do."

### Step 3: Follow the Student's Lead
Avoid pushing for disclosure or pursuing a line of inquiry the student is reluctant to follow. If a student says "I don't want to talk about that right now," respect it. Say: "That's completely okay. We don't have to go there. What would be most useful to talk about today?"

### Step 4: Avoid Retraumatization
Do not ask students to repeat distressing information multiple times across different advisors. Coordinate with colleagues so the student has one primary point of contact. When documentation is needed, explain why and let the student know who will see it.

### Step 5: Validate and Normalize
Responses like shame, avoidance, and anger are normal trauma responses. When a student is visibly distressed or behaves in ways that might seem defensive, name it gently: "It makes complete sense that this is hard to talk about. A lot of people feel that way."

## Warning Signs (that trauma may be present)
- Student becomes visibly distressed or dissociated when discussing certain topics
- Student has significant difficulty trusting authority figures
- Student reacts with intense anger or distress to relatively minor academic concerns
- Student describes a history of adverse childhood experiences when asked
- Student has difficulty concentrating, sleeping, or self-regulating emotions
- Student has a pattern of withdrawing from support at key moments

## Escalation Triggers
- Student discloses ongoing abuse or domestic violence
- Student shows signs of acute PTSD (flashbacks, severe hyperarousal, inability to function)
- Student is at risk of harm related to their traumatic history

## Sample Language / Scripts
- "I want to make sure this feels like a safe space. You can share as much or as little as you want today."
- "I can hear that this is really difficult. You don't have to explain everything — I just want you to know I'm here."
- "Whatever you've been through, it doesn't define your ability to succeed here. We'll work through this together."
- "I'm going to note that you mentioned [X] — but I want you to know I won't share that without talking to you first."
