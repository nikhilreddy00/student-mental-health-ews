# Supporting Students with Anxiety and Depression

## Overview
Anxiety and depression are the two most common mental health difficulties among university students. Research consistently shows that 1 in 4 students experiences a mental health problem during their studies, with anxiety and depression accounting for the majority of presentations. Many students experience both simultaneously. These conditions are treatable, but left unsupported they significantly impair academic performance, social engagement, and wellbeing.

Advisors are not clinicians, but they are often the first person a student discloses to. Understanding the signs, knowing how to respond compassionately, and making effective referrals can make a critical difference.

## Key Principles
- Anxiety and depression are medical conditions, not character weaknesses
- Stigma is real — many students are reluctant to disclose or seek help
- Both conditions respond well to early intervention
- The academic environment can trigger, maintain, and exacerbate both conditions
- Small, consistent support from an advisor can significantly buffer the impact of both conditions while specialist help is being sought

## Step-by-Step Guidance

### Step 1: Recognize the Presentation
Anxiety may present as perfectionism, excessive worry about grades, avoidance of assessments or presentations, frequent reassurance-seeking, or visible physical tension. Depression may present as withdrawal, flat affect, declining self-care, hopelessness about outcomes, or unexplained absence. Some students present with irritability or anger rather than sadness.

### Step 2: Create Space for Disclosure
Students rarely self-identify as having anxiety or depression without being asked. Open the conversation: "A lot of students find this time of year really difficult. How are you finding it yourself?" If the student mentions stress or difficulty concentrating, gently explore further: "How long have you been feeling this way?"

### Step 3: Validate Without Over-Medicalizing
When a student discloses anxiety or depression, validate their experience without immediately labeling it as an illness: "What you're describing sounds really hard. A lot of people experience something similar, and there's a lot we can do." Referral to counseling or health services can be introduced naturally from here.

### Step 4: Offer Practical Academic Support
Anxiety and depression affect cognitive function — concentration, memory, and motivation are all impaired. Work with the module team to explore practical adjustments: assessment deadline extensions, reduced assessment load temporarily, or alternative submission formats. Disability Services can formalize these arrangements.

### Step 5: Monitor and Follow Up
Students with anxiety and depression need consistent, predictable contact. Erratic support — intensive during a crisis, then absent — can worsen both conditions. Agree a regular check-in schedule (fortnightly or monthly) and stick to it even when the student seems fine.

## Warning Signs
- Student reports persistent sleep difficulties (too much or too little)
- Student expresses hopelessness about their academic future
- Student has stopped engaging in activities they previously enjoyed
- Student is showing clear physical signs of anxiety (trembling, breathing changes, visible distress before assessments)
- Student reports persistent physical symptoms without clear medical cause (headaches, stomach problems)
- Student becomes tearful when discussing academic topics
- Student expresses feelings of worthlessness or excessive self-blame

## Escalation Triggers
- Student expresses suicidal thoughts alongside depression
- Student's functioning has deteriorated to the point where they cannot care for themselves
- Student discloses that anxiety or depression has been present for months with no improvement
- Student is resistant to all referrals but clearly needs clinical support

## Sample Language / Scripts
- "It sounds like you're carrying a lot right now. How long have things been feeling this way?"
- "What you're describing — the difficulty concentrating, the worry about everything going wrong — is something a lot of students experience. And it is something that can get better with the right support."
- "I'm not a counselor, and I want to make sure you're talking to someone who really knows how to help with this. Would you be open to making an appointment with the University Counseling Service?"
- "Even while you're waiting to see a counselor, let's think about what we can do about your assessments. You don't have to manage both the stress and the academic pressure at the same time."

## Resources and Referrals
- University Counseling Service: primary referral for anxiety and depression
- Student Health Centre: for GP referral, medication assessment, and sick notes
- Disability Services: for formal academic adjustments if diagnosis confirmed
- Mindfulness / Wellbeing Programmes: self-help resources while waiting for counseling
- Crisis Support: Samaritans 116 123, Crisis Text Line (text HELLO to 85258)
