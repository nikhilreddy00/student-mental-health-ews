# First Contact Scripts for Advisors

## Overview
The first contact with a student who may be struggling is the most critical — and most difficult — part of the support process. Done well, it opens a relationship. Done poorly, it closes one. Advisors often worry about saying the wrong thing or making the situation worse. This guide provides tested, evidence-based scripts for common first-contact scenarios.

The underlying principle across all scripts is the same: lead with genuine care, create a low-pressure opening, and make it easy for the student to respond without feeling judged, embarrassed, or obligated.

## Key Principles
- Specific beats generic — reference something real about the student's situation
- Warmth beats formality — emails that sound like policy notices get ignored
- Short beats long — students in distress rarely read long messages
- Options beat demands — give the student a choice of how to respond
- Never lead with consequences in the first message

## Step-by-Step Guidance

### Step 1: Choose the Right Channel
Email is appropriate for initial low-urgency contact. Phone or text is better for urgent situations or when email has gone unanswered. In-person is best for students you already have a relationship with. Avoid messaging through formal university portals for first contact — they feel bureaucratic.

### Step 2: Personalize the Opening
The first sentence should make clear this is not a mass communication. Reference the student by name and reference something specific: their course, their last known interaction, or a specific concern.

### Step 3: Name the Purpose Clearly and Kindly
State why you're reaching out without euphemism, but without alarm. "I noticed..." and "I wanted to check in..." are proven openers that don't threaten or pressure.

### Step 4: Offer Two Low-Barrier Response Options
Give the student choice in how they respond. This reduces anxiety and increases the likelihood of reply. "You can reply to this email, or if you'd prefer to chat, I'm happy to find a time that works for you."

### Step 5: Close with Genuine Care
End with a statement that removes any sense of obligation while reinforcing that you care. "There's no pressure — I just want you to know you have support if you need it."

## Warning Signs (in student responses)
- Replies that are unusually brief or dismissive ("I'm fine thanks")
- Responses that deflect but don't reassure
- No response after two attempts
- Responses that mention stress but minimize it ("just a bit busy")

## Sample Language / Scripts

### Script A — Engagement Drop (Email)
Subject: Checking in — Student [Name]

"Hi [Name], I noticed you haven't been on the course recently, and I wanted to reach out personally to see how you're doing. There's absolutely nothing to worry about — I'm not writing about deadlines or grades. I just wanted to make sure you're okay. If there's anything going on that I can help with, or if you'd just like to talk, I'm here. You can reply here or we can find a time for a quick call if that's easier. Take care."

### Script B — Assessment Missed (Email)
"Hi [Name], I saw you didn't submit the recent assessment, and I wanted to check in. I know life can get complicated, and I'm not writing to chase you about marks. I'm writing because I care about how you're doing. If something's come up, I'd love to hear about it — there may be options we can explore together. Just reply here whenever you're ready."

### Script C — Phone Follow-up After No Email Response
"Hi [Name], this is [Advisor Name] from [University]. I sent you an email last week and just wanted to follow up to make sure you're okay. There's no pressure here — I just wanted to hear your voice and know you're alright. If now isn't a good time, feel free to call me back at [number] or drop me an email. I'm easy to reach."

### Script D — In-person Opening (Walk-in or Drop-in)
"Hi [Name], thanks for coming in. I want you to know this is a relaxed conversation — there's nothing formal happening. I just noticed [specific observation] and thought it would be nice to catch up. How have things been going for you lately?"
